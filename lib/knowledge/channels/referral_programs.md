# Referral Programs — Implementation Guide

## When to Recommend
Any business where word-of-mouth already drives some customers. Highest impact for: fitness studios (30-40% of new members from referrals at top studios), professional services (trust-driven purchases), restaurants (repeat visits), and local services (neighborhood networks). Lower impact for e-commerce (referral programs exist but compete with discount culture).

## Structure by Business Type

### Fitness / Wellness Studios
**What works:** "Bring a friend, you both get something." The incentive must benefit both parties — one-sided referral bonuses feel transactional.
- **Offer:** Referring member gets one free month (or $50 credit). Friend gets first month at 50% off (not free — free devalues the membership).
- **Mechanism:** Printed referral cards at the front desk + a unique referral link per member via email. Track which members refer and recognize the top referrers publicly (leaderboard, shout-out in class).
- **Timing:** Launch during a "Bring a Friend Week" event. Creates urgency and social proof (everyone's inviting someone, not just one person).
- **Target:** 15-20% of new members from referrals within 6 months. Below 10% means the program isn't visible enough or the incentive isn't motivating.

### Restaurants
**What works:** Referral programs that feel like hospitality, not marketing.
- **Offer:** "Buy a friend their first drink" — the referring customer gets a $15 credit to send a friend a free appetizer or cocktail. Digital gift card via text/email.
- **Mechanism:** Integrate with the POS system if possible. Otherwise, a simple tracking spreadsheet works. The key is making it easy: a link the customer can text to a friend, not a form to fill out.
- **Timing:** Launch post-visit. After a customer pays, the server mentions: "If you enjoyed tonight, send a friend on us" and hands them a card with a QR code.
- **Target:** 5-10% of new customers from referrals. Restaurant referral programs have lower conversion rates than fitness because the ask is less personal — but the customer acquisition cost is near-zero.

### Professional Services
**What works:** Referral as professional courtesy, not discount marketing. Lawyers, therapists, and consultants don't discount — they reward.
- **Offer:** For the referrer: a handwritten thank-you note + a $100 gift card (not a service discount — that cheapens the relationship). For the referred: priority scheduling or a complimentary initial consultation.
- **Mechanism:** Email existing clients once per quarter: "If you know someone who could benefit from [specific service], I'd welcome the introduction." Personal, not mass-blast. Include 2-3 sentences about who you work best with — this focuses the referral.
- **Timing:** Ask for referrals after a win. When a case settles, a tax return comes back favorable, or a therapy milestone is reached — that's when the client is most satisfied and most likely to refer.
- **Target:** 20-30% of new clients from referrals in an established practice. Below 10% means clients are satisfied but not activated. The ask hasn't been made.

### Local Home Services
**What works:** Neighborhood-based referral networks. A homeowner who had a great plumber tells three neighbors.
- **Offer:** $50 credit toward next service for each referral that books. The referred neighbor gets $25 off their first call. Keep amounts proportional to job value — $50 off a $300+ service call is meaningful without being expensive.
- **Mechanism:** Leave 3 referral cards after every completed job. Business card size with a unique code or QR link. "Know a neighbor who needs [service]? $25 off for them, $50 credit for you."
- **Timing:** At the moment of maximum satisfaction — when the job is done, the mess is cleaned up, and the problem is solved. Not in a follow-up email two weeks later.
- **Target:** 10-15% of new jobs from referrals. Home services referrals are highly seasonal — spring and fall are peak.

## What Good Looks Like
- **Referral rate:** Percentage of new customers who were referred. Track this monthly. Upward trend means the program is working.
- **Cost per referral acquisition:** Should be 30-50% lower than paid acquisition. If it's not, the incentive is too expensive or the program is attracting low-quality referrals.
- **Referral-to-conversion rate:** What percentage of referred leads actually become customers? Should be 40-60% — much higher than cold leads because trust transfers.
- **Net Promoter Score (NPS) correlation:** Businesses with high NPS (customers who would recommend you) but low referral rates have an activation problem, not a satisfaction problem. The program just needs to be built.

## Common Mistakes the Plan Should Flag
- **Relying on organic word-of-mouth without structure.** "Our customers refer us all the time" means some do, randomly. A structured program doubles or triples the rate by making it easy and rewarding.
- **Incentives that are too small.** A $10 discount doesn't motivate anyone to have an awkward conversation recommending a plumber. $50 does. The referral incentive should be meaningful enough to remember.
- **No tracking.** "We think most customers come from referrals" is a guess. If you can't attribute referrals to specific customers, you can't optimize the program or reward top referrers.
- **One-time launch with no follow-up.** Referral programs decay without reinforcement. Remind customers every quarter. Celebrate referrers. Keep the program visible — front desk signage, email footer, invoice insert.
