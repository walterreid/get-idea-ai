# Fitness & Wellness (Studios, Gyms, Personal Training, Yoga)

## What Actually Converts

### 1. Google Business Profile + Local Search
The unglamorous truth: most gym memberships start with a Google search, not an Instagram post. "Gym near me," "yoga studio [neighborhood]," "CrossFit [city]" — these searches drive 60-70% of first visits for independent fitness businesses. A GBP with 80+ reviews, current class schedules, and fresh photos will outperform a $1,000/month Instagram ad budget.

**The radius matters:** Fitness is hyper-local. Members won't drive more than 12 minutes. Target a 3-5 mile radius in urban areas, 8-10 in suburban. If you're spending money reaching people outside that radius, you're wasting it.

### 2. Google Ads (Local Services + Search)
For personal trainers and specialty studios (Pilates, barre, strength training), Google Ads targeting "[specialty] [neighborhood]" converts well because the searcher already knows what they want. Keep budgets focused on the immediate service area. Cost-per-lead runs $15-30 for fitness, which means every lead needs to convert at 25%+ to make the math work on a $100-200/month membership.

**The trial offer hook:** Google Ads work best when they drive to a specific offer — "First week free" or "$29 intro month" — not the homepage. The ad-to-landing-page-to-offer pipeline matters more than the ad copy itself.

### 3. Referral Programs
The highest-converting channel in fitness is word of mouth, and most studios leave it completely unstructured. A formal referral program ("Bring a friend, you both get a free month") consistently outperforms any paid channel on cost-per-acquisition. The best studios get 30-40% of new members from referrals. If you're getting under 15%, the program is broken or nonexistent.

## What Wastes Money

**Boosted Instagram Posts:** The most common mistake in fitness marketing. Owners see engagement (likes, comments) and think it's working. But engagement on a workout video doesn't convert to memberships. A boosted post reaching 10,000 people might generate 2-3 inquiries. That same $150 on Google Ads generates 5-10 leads from people actively searching for a gym.

**Instagram ads targeting broad "fitness enthusiasts":** You're competing with Peloton, Nike, and every supplement brand. Your $500/month is noise. Instagram organic (daily class clips, member transformations, community moments) is valuable for retention and culture — but paid Instagram for acquisition is a money pit for studios under $500K revenue.

**ClassPass / discount aggregators:** ClassPass fills empty spots at $3-5 per visit when your per-visit revenue needs to be $15-25. It trains customers to pay discount rates and shop on price. Unless you're genuinely empty and need bodies to build energy, avoid it. The customers it sends rarely convert to full memberships.

**Print/local magazine ads:** Still common in fitness, still ineffective. A $400 quarter-page ad in a local magazine reaches people who aren't looking for a gym. That's awareness spending disguised as lead generation.

## Budget Tiers

- **$500/mo:** Google Ads targeting [specialty] + [neighborhood] ($350) + GBP optimization with weekly photo posts ($150 in time/tools). No Instagram spend. Build referral program manually.
- **$2,000/mo:** Google Ads ($1,000) + formal referral program with incentives ($300) + professional content for social/GBP ($400) + email marketing to leads and former members ($300).
- **$5,000/mo:** Google Ads ($2,000) + retargeting website visitors ($500) + community events/challenges as marketing ($800) + professional content creation ($700) + email/SMS automation ($500) + SEO for class-specific pages ($500).

## The One Mistake Everyone Makes

**Competing on price instead of identity.** Independent studios can't beat Planet Fitness on price and shouldn't try. But owners panic when a $10/month chain opens nearby and start discounting. The plan should help them articulate what makes their community different — and put that identity in front of the people searching for it. A strength training gym doesn't compete with Planet Fitness any more than a steakhouse competes with McDonald's. The messaging needs to make that clear.

## When Research Is Thin

If the studio has minimal web presence, the structural problem is almost always: (1) few Google reviews (under 30), (2) no clear differentiation on the website ("we offer group fitness, personal training, and nutrition coaching" — so does everyone), and (3) spending on Instagram instead of Google. The diagnosis should name what makes this studio's community different from the 15 other options within 5 miles — and build the marketing plan around that identity, not around generic "fitness" keywords.
