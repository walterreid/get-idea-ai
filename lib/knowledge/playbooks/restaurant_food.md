# Restaurants & Food Service

## What Actually Converts

### 1. Google Business Profile (GBP)
For restaurants, GBP isn't a marketing channel — it's the front door. 70%+ of restaurant discovery happens through Google Maps and the local 3-pack. A restaurant with 200 reviews at 4.3 stars will pull more covers than one spending $2,000/month on Instagram with 15 reviews at 4.8. Volume of social proof matters more than perfection.

**What separates winners:** Menu posted and current. Photos uploaded weekly (real food shots from service, not a photoshoot from 2019). Hours accurate, including holiday hours. Responding to negative reviews within 24 hours with a real human response — not "We're sorry you had this experience." Google rewards completeness and recency.

### 2. Instagram (Organic, Not Ads)
The one category where Instagram organic actually works — because food is visual and shareable. But organic, not paid. A restaurant spending $500/month boosting posts is wasting money compared to one posting 4-5 times per week with real kitchen/service photos and building a following through consistency.

**The content that converts:** Behind-the-scenes kitchen shots, daily specials with timestamps, staff moments, the ugly dish that tastes incredible. Not polished marketing — real, frequent, human. User-generated content (reposting customer photos) builds community at zero cost.

### 3. Google Search Ads (Specific Queries Only)
Only cost-effective for specific, high-intent searches: "private dining room [city]," "catering [neighborhood]," "brunch reservation [area]." Not worth bidding on "restaurants near me" — the competition is too broad and GBP handles that organically if the profile is strong.

## What Wastes Money

**Facebook Ads for foot traffic:** The targeting sounds good ("foodies within 5 miles") but the conversion tracking is nearly impossible. You can't attribute a Tuesday dinner to a Facebook ad someone saw Saturday. The exceptions: event promotion (wine dinner, live music) and grand openings. Everything else is vanity metrics.

**Influencer partnerships (unpaid):** "Free meal for a post" attracts low-value influencers with bought followers. If you're going to do influencer marketing, pay for it, negotiate deliverables, and pick local food bloggers with actual engagement — not lifestyle accounts.

**Yelp Ads:** Restaurants live and die by Yelp reviews, but Yelp's ad product cannibalizes your own organic listing. You're paying to appear above yourself. Invest the same money in training staff to ask happy customers to leave a Yelp review instead.

**Broad SEO campaigns:** Restaurants don't need to rank for "Italian restaurant." They need to rank for their name, their neighborhood, and their signature dish. That's GBP, not a $2,000/month SEO retainer.

## Budget Tiers

- **$500/mo:** GBP optimization (weekly posts, review management) + basic photography equipment for staff to shoot daily content. Zero paid ads. This is the right answer for most independent restaurants.
- **$2,000/mo:** GBP management ($300) + Google Ads for specific revenue streams like catering/events ($700) + professional monthly content shoot ($500) + targeted email/SMS for repeat customers ($500).
- **$5,000/mo:** Above + event-based Facebook campaigns ($1,000) + local media/PR push ($1,500) + loyalty program infrastructure ($500). Only justified for restaurants with $1M+ annual revenue or multiple locations.

## The One Mistake Everyone Makes

**Chasing new customers instead of activating existing ones.** A restaurant with 100 covers/week doesn't need more awareness — it needs those 100 people to come back more often and bring friends. Email/SMS list from the POS system, a simple "come back" offer, and consistently great GBP presence will grow revenue more than any ad campaign. New customer acquisition should come AFTER the repeat business engine is working.

## When Research Is Thin

If you can't find the restaurant online, that IS the diagnosis. They're invisible on Google Maps, which means they're invisible to everyone searching for where to eat. The plan starts with "get your GBP claimed, complete, and active" before any other recommendation makes sense. Ask if they have a POS system with customer data — that's their most valuable marketing asset and they probably don't know it.
